# SPERB Tier Boundaries — Full Reference

This document explains the five SPERB tiers, their score boundaries, their practical implications, and the reasoning behind the tier design.

---

## Tier Overview

| Tier | Symbol | Score Range | One-Line Meaning |
|------|--------|------------|-----------------|
| Platinum | 🏆 | 85–100 | Governance leader — sets the standard |
| Gold | 🥇 | 70–84 | Ethical practitioner — trustworthy partner |
| Silver | 🥈 | 55–69 | Partially compliant — engage with due diligence |
| Bronze | 🥉 | 40–54 | Minimal compliance — significant concerns |
| Unrated | ⛔ | Below 40 | Non-compliant / at risk |

---

## Platinum — 85 to 100

**What it means:** The entity operates with governance infrastructure that is not merely compliant but constitutive — it sets the practice that others should follow. Platinum-tier entities are positioned as templates for industry adoption.

**What it requires in practice:**
- Full public disclosure on all platforms (AIDS 8+)
- Formal governance documentation (GDS 8+)
- Clean conduct record with active monitoring (ECS 8+)
- Documented emotional boundary policy (SCES 8+)
- Competitive photorealism (PVS 7+)
- Scores across all other dimensions that reflect active, documented care

**Brand implication:** Partner without qualification. The entity's governance can be cited in your own compliance documentation.

**Platform implication:** Featured in responsible AI creator programmes. Eligible for governance-leader badges.

**Regulatory implication:** Model entity for compliance frameworks.

**Inaugural example:** Shayari NHE-01 — 95/100

---

## Gold — 70 to 84

**What it means:** The entity meets or exceeds baseline ethical expectations across most dimensions. It has not yet reached governance-leader status — typically because formal documentation is absent — but its ongoing practice has accumulated into a defensible ethical record.

**What it requires in practice:**
- Bio-level disclosure on all platforms (AIDS 7+)
- Named creator and studio (GDS 3–6)
- No documented ethical violations (ECS 7+)
- No active romantic simulation (SCES 5+)
- Competitive photorealism (PVS 6+)

**Brand implication:** Partner with confidence. Due diligence recommended but does not require escalation.

**Platform implication:** Eligible for verified AI creator status.

**Regulatory implication:** Compliant with most current regulations; minor documentation gaps may exist.

**Inaugural examples:** Imma (80), Noonoouri (80), Kenza Layli (77), Rozy (75)

---

## Silver — 55 to 69

**What it means:** The entity meets disclosure and conduct minimums but exhibits notable gaps in governance, parasocial design, or commercial transparency. Silver-tier entities are commercially operable but require explicit due diligence before partnership.

**What it requires in practice:**
- Some form of AI disclosure (AIDS 5+)
- Creator identifiable (GDS 2+)
- No major conduct violations (ECS 5+)
- Photorealism adequate (PVS 5+)
- Notable gaps in at least 2–3 other dimensions

**Brand implication:** Partner with documented due diligence. Document the SPERB gaps in your partnership risk assessment. Require remediation of critical dimensions (AIDS, GDS) before campaign activation.

**Platform implication:** Standard creator status. Enhanced monitoring recommended.

**Regulatory implication:** Disclosure gaps may create compliance exposure in regulated markets.

**Inaugural examples:** Aitana Lopez (69), Shudu Gram (68), Lil Miquela (66), Kyra (63), Naina Avtr (63)

---

## Bronze — 40 to 54

**What it means:** The entity meets the floor of recognisable operation but exhibits substantive concerns across multiple dimensions. Bronze-tier entities should not be commercially engaged without a mitigation plan.

**What it requires in practice:**
- Minimal disclosure present somewhere (AIDS 2–4)
- Some identifiable operator (GDS 1–3)
- No catastrophic conduct violations (ECS 3+)
- At least some original content (CPOS 3+)

**Brand implication:** Do not partner without explicit mitigation of the specific SPERB gaps. Require remediation to Silver tier before campaign activation.

**Platform implication:** Flagged for enhanced review. Monetisation access conditional on remediation.

**Regulatory implication:** Material compliance risk in most jurisdictions.

---

## Unrated — Below 40

**What it means:** The entity falls below the threshold of legitimate operation under SPERB criteria. The "Unrated" designation is deliberately not called "Failing" — it positions the entity as outside the scope of evaluable legitimate operation rather than merely a poor performer within it.

**What it characterises:**
- No disclosure or active concealment (AIDS 1–2)
- Anonymous operator (GDS 1)
- Documented violations (ECS 1–3)
- IP integrity concerns (CPOS 1–2)
- Typically also: cultural exploitation and commercial deception

**Brand implication:** Non-engagement advised. Any partnership creates co-liability for the entity's conduct and disclosure failures.

**Platform implication:** Flagged for compliance review. Content labelling enforcement initiated.

**Regulatory implication:** Likely in violation of applicable disclosure regulations. Enforcement referral may be appropriate.

**Inaugural reference:** Tanvi Joshi — 21/100 (cautionary calibration floor, not a cohort member)

## The Asymmetry Design

SPERB tier consequences are intentionally asymmetric:

**Platinum** → public asset, commercial advantage, governance template
**Unrated** → public warning, elevated due diligence, grounds for non-engagement

This asymmetry exists because the framework's purpose is to make virtuous operation legible and harmful operation visible. The reward for governance leadership is commercial and reputational advantage. The cost of non-compliance is public visibility of that non-compliance.

An entity that does not want to be evaluated can choose not to engage with SPERB. But in a market where comparable entities publish their scores, the absence of a score is itself a signal.

---

## Why These Specific Boundaries?

The tier boundaries (40, 55, 70, 85) were calibrated against the inaugural pilot cohort to ensure meaningful differentiation:

- The Tanvi Joshi reference (21/100) falls well within Unrated — correctly, since it represents the field's most documented case of catastrophic ethical failure
- The Gold-Silver boundary at 70 captures the clear distinction between entities with named operators and consistent disclosure (Gold) and those with bio-level-only disclosure and significant governance gaps (Silver)
- The Platinum boundary at 85 is deliberately high — it requires strong performance across all eight dimensions, not just the disclosure and conduct dimensions
